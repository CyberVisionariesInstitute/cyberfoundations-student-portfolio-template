# Start Here

If this is your first time using GitHub, welcome — you're in the right place, and you don't need to know anything yet. This page is your first lab, and it doubles as your first real commit.

## What Just Happened?

If you got to this repo by clicking **Use this template** on `cyberfoundations-student-portfolio-template`, you now have your own independent copy of this structure. It is yours. Nothing you do here affects the original template, and nothing anyone else does affects you.

If you haven't done that yet, stop here and go back to your Week 1 lesson in Circle for the exact steps — you'll want your own copy before continuing.

## Your First Commit

This takes about 10 minutes and puts your name on the internet as someone building real technical skills.

### Step 1 — Open Your README

Click on `README.md` in this repo, then click the pencil icon (Edit) in the top right of the file view.

### Step 2 — Write About Yourself

At the top of the file, add a short section about you. Answer:

- Who are you?
- Why did you sign up for Cyber Foundations?
- What do you hope to do with these skills?

Three honest sentences is plenty. You do not need to write perfectly.

### Step 3 — Commit Your Change

Scroll to the bottom of the edit page. In the **Commit changes** box, leave the default message or write something like `Add intro to README`. Click **Commit changes**.

That's it. Your change is saved, live, and permanently part of your repo's history.

## What Does "Commit" Actually Mean?

A commit is like hitting Save — except GitHub keeps a record of every save you ever make, with a timestamp and a message describing what changed. You can always go back and see exactly what changed and when. Every commit you make in this course adds to your professional history.

## Where Things Go From Here
